The story unfolds in the Wiltshire countryside, where the protagonist rents a modest cottage on the grounds of a decaying manor estate owned by a reclusive landlord named Mr. Phillips. This setting becomes a microcosm for broader themes. The narrator, a thinly veiled version of Naipaul, recovering from illness and the exhaustion of his earlier travels, immerses himself in the rhythms of rural life. 

He observes the changing seasons, the estate's crumbling grandeur, and the lives of its inhabitants: the eccentric landlord, the devoted but aging gardener Jack, the housekeeper Mrs. Phillips, and other peripheral figures like the farm laborers and visitors. Through these observations, Naipaul paints a vivid portrait of England's pastoral idyll as both enchanting and illusory, marked by entropy and the inexorable passage of time.

Central to the novel is the theme of arrival—not just physical, but existential. The title likely alludes to Giorgio de Chirico's surrealist painting The Enigma of Arrival, which depicts a mysterious figure approaching a walled city, evoking a sense of anticipation and alienation. The narrator reflects on his own "arrival" in England, the former colonial metropole, after leaving Trinidad. He recounts his childhood in a fragmented, postcolonial society, his scholarship to Oxford, and his struggles as a writer in London during the 1950s and 1960s. These recollections intertwine with his present, revealing how his perceptions of England have evolved from idealized fantasy to a more nuanced understanding of its decline. The empire's fading glory mirrors the estate's decay, symbolizing the broader unraveling of colonial legacies and personal aspirations.

Naipaul's prose is meticulous and elegiac, rich with sensory details—the mist-shrouded valleys, the blooming gardens, the sounds of wildlife—that underscore themes of impermanence and renewal. The narrator's walks through the landscape prompt philosophical musings on art, history, and identity. He grapples with his outsider status as a postcolonial migrant, questioning the myths of cultural superiority and the artist's role in interpreting a changing world. Figures like Jack, who tends his garden with quiet dignity until his death, embody a stoic acceptance of life's cycles, contrasting with the narrator's restless introspection.

Throughout, Naipaul explores the "enigma" of human existence: the gap between expectation and reality, the search for meaning amid flux. The novel critiques romantic notions of rural England, exposing underlying social tensions, class divides, and the encroachment of modernity. Yet it also celebrates moments of epiphany, as the narrator achieves a tentative sense of belonging through writing and observation.
